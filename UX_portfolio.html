Product Design Projects

Latency Lock

This project was created through careful user-centered product design in my Mechanical Design and Fabrication class. My team and I created a list of challenges that students on the Tufts campus face daily by observing students on campus.

Through dot voting with sticky notes, we narrowed the challenges to one main challenge and framed a design problem. We then created a stakeholder map to identify everyone who is impacted by our design problem and map out their relationships.

Design problem statement: "College students who live in dorms have the problem of getting locked out by forgetting their IDs when leaving their dorm. Our solution should deliver a reliable, non-damaging, and easy to use method of preventing lockouts."

In order to understand who our users are and what they need, we carefully created an unbiased survey on Qualtrics and received approximately 250 responses. We analyzed the data recevied from the survey, created a user profile for similar groups of people or demographics and used an affinity diagram to organize these groups. After reviewing our user profile groups, we created personas to help us better understand potential users affected by our problem statement.

To get an even deeper understanding of our potential user, we performed a task analysis to understand what our users' goals are, what they are trying to achieve, and what they usually do to achieve these goals.

Then, we identified user needs and engineering requirements for our device. To organize these needs and requirements by importance, we used another affinity diagram. To then understand what needs were satisfied by others, we did some benchmarking using a market research and information research on current products on the market.

Finally, after completing ideation techniques like mind mapping, function structures and brainwriting 6-3-5, we began our prototyping and fabrication process. We used Computer Aided-Design, 3D printing, laser cutting, drilling, circuits and motors, metal cutting, and an Arduino to create our artifact.

In the end, we created a device that lowered a thin piece of metal in between a door frame and the tongue of the door with a Servo motor to prevent the door from closing for a set amount of time. This allowed for a grace period for the user to realize they forgot their key to their dorm room, and to come back into the room and retreive it before the door locked. The user was able to set a preferred amount of time for this grace period using a pedometer. The device was activated using a touch sensor as the user was exiting their dorm room, and when the grace period was over, the Servo motor would remove the metal piece from the door frame. This device is able to attach to the wall on the inside of the user's room.

Pinball Machine

This pinball machine was created through my Electrical Engineering for Mechanical Engineers class here at Tufts for a project. My partner and I used 3D printing to create the obstacles on the board. We also used lazer cutting to create the board, board walls, and standing legs.

In order to add up points, we had to get creative and use copper tape and a small metal ball to complete the circuit and send an electrical signal to an Arduino. Once this Arduino received the signal, it sent this information to our small display and added a point onto the user's score. The display showed the score to the user at all times while playing the game, and updated the score whenever the user hit an obstacle with copper tape. Additionally, we were able to connect a Servo motor to the pin wheel to spin the wheel at a constant speed. We also used a small Solenoid to move up and down on the board through a drill-cut hole to add more difficulty in playing the game. The levers to push the ball back up onto the board were actually door stops that had a spring- like body to add momentum easily to the levers.
